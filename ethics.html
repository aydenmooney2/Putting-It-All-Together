What is Bias, and how does is develop?

Bias refers to a tendency to favor certain perspectives, ideas, or groups over others, often in a way that is not fully objective or fair. It can exist at both an individual and systemic level, shaping how people interpret information, make decisions, and interact with the world. Bias develops through a combination of cognitive processes, personal experiences, cultural influences, and the design of the systems people use. From a psychological perspective, humans naturally rely on mental shortcuts, or heuristics, to process large amounts of information quickly; while these shortcuts are efficient, they can lead to patterns of thinking that reinforce preexisting beliefs and assumptions. Over time, repeated exposure to similar viewpoints—whether through upbringing, education, or media consumption—can strengthen these biases, making them feel like objective truth rather than subjective interpretation. Social and cultural environments also play a significant role, as individuals are influenced by the norms, values, and narratives present in their communities, which can shape attitudes toward different groups or ideas. In digital environments, bias can be further amplified by system design and information structures. Algorithms and content delivery systems often prioritize engagement, meaning users are more likely to see information that aligns with their existing beliefs, creating echo chambers that reinforce bias rather than challenge it. This connects to the concept of information disorder, where misleading or one-sided content spreads easily and influences perception (Wardle & Derakhshan, 2017). Additionally, design principles discussed by Nielsen (1994) and Norman (2013) highlight that users tend to trust systems that appear intuitive and authoritative, even if the underlying information is flawed, which can unintentionally reinforce biased thinking. Garrett (2010) further emphasizes that how information is structured and presented affects how users interpret and prioritize it, meaning bias can be embedded not just in content but in the way systems organize and display information. Bias can also be introduced at the system level through data, particularly in automated or AI-driven systems; if the data used to train these systems reflects existing inequalities or skewed perspectives, the outputs will likely reproduce and even amplify those biases. This is why frameworks like those from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (2020) stress the importance of integrity, transparency, and accountability in system design. Ultimately, bias develops gradually through repeated exposure, reinforced beliefs, and the environments—both social and technological—that individuals engage with. It is not always intentional, but it can have significant impacts on perception, decision-making, and fairness. Understanding how bias forms is important because it allows individuals and organizations to recognize its presence, question assumptions, and design systems and practices that promote more balanced, inclusive, and accurate outcomes.

Best Practices

  • Look at more than one source, from different sides of the story.
  • Look for factual points; numbers, times, patterns, etc.
  • Avoid biased statements or persuasive points, and single-sided perspectives.
  • Use Primary and Secondary sources.
